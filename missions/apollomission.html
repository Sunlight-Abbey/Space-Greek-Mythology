Apollo Mission

Mission

In 1961, President John F. Kennedy challenged America to get a man on the moon and return him to Earth safely before the end of the 1960's. In order to fulfill this, NASA created the Apollo Program. America wanted to show superiority in space and technology, especially over the Soviet Union. This task was completed in 1969 with the Apollo 11 Mission, successfully landing Neil Armstrong on the moon.

Mythology

The name for this mission actually comes from a misconception. NASA wanted to name the mission after the greek god of the sun, who drives his chariot across it. However, there are two errors with this. First, the sun god does not drive his chariot across the sun, but across the Earth. His chariot is the sun itself. Second, a very popular misconception, Apollo does not drive the sun chariot. Apollo is simply the god of the sunlight, while Helios represents the sun himself. In most greek mythology, Helios is the driver of the chariot. The most popular instance when Apollo drives it is in the Metamorphoses by Ovid, who was actually not greek, but roman.
